**Carl Philipp Gottfried** (ou **Gottlieb** ) **von Clausewitz** <sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;">[</font></font></span><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"> note 1 </font></font><span class="cite-bracket"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;">]</font></font></span></a></sup> ( [/ ˈ k l aʊ z ə v ɪ t s / KLOW](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English "Aide : API/anglais") [*\-zə -vits*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Aide : Clé de réorthographe de la prononciation") , allemand : [\[ˈkaʁl fɔn ˈklaʊzəvɪts\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German "Aide : API/allemand standard") <sup class="ext-phonos-attribution noexcerpt navigation-not-searchable"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:De-Carl_von_Clausewitz.ogg" title="Fichier:De-Carl von Clausewitz.ogg"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;">ⓘ</font></font></a></sup> ; 1er juillet 1780 – 16 novembre 1831) <sup id="cite_ref-Clausewitzstudies.org_2-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-Clausewitzstudies.org-2"><span class="cite-bracket"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"> [</font></font></span><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"> 1 </font></font><span class="cite-bracket"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;">]</font></font></span></a></sup> était ungénéral et[théoricien militaire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_theory "théorie militaire")[prussien](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia "Royaume de Prusse")qui mettait l'accent sur les aspects « moraux » (en termes modernes,[psychologiques](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_psychology "psychologie militaire")) et politiques de la conduite de la guerre. Son ouvrage le plus remarquable, *[Vom Kriege](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vom_Kriege "De la guerre")* (*De la guerre*), bien qu'inachevé à sa mort, est considéré comme un traité fondateur de[stratégie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_strategy "stratégie militaire")et[science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_science "science militaire").**Clausewitz a souligné l'interaction multiple de divers facteurs en temps de guerre, soulignant comment les événements inattendus se déroulant dans le « [brouillard de la guerre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fog_of_war "Brouillard de guerre") » (c'est-à-dire face à des informations incomplètes, douteuses et souvent erronées, et à une grande [peur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fear "Peur") , au doute et à l'excitation) exigent des décisions rapides de la part de commandants vigilants. Il considérait l'histoire comme un moyen essentiel de vérifier les abstractions érudites qui ne concordaient pas avec l'expérience. Contrairement aux premiers travaux d' [Antoine-Henri Jomini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antoine-Henri_Jomini "Antoine-Henri Jomini") , il soutenait que la guerre ne pouvait être quantifiée ni réduite à des cartes, de la géométrie et des graphiques. Clausewitz avait de nombreux [aphorismes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aphorism "Aphorisme") , dont l'un des plus célèbres est : « La guerre est la continuation de la politique par d'autres moyens. » <sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-:0-3"><span class="cite-bracket"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;">[</font></font></span><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"> 2 </font></font><span class="cite-bracket"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;">]</font></font></span></a></sup> <sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page&nbsp;: 87"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;">&nbsp;: 87 </font></font></span></sup> 

## Nom

\[ [modifier](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carl_von_Clausewitz&action=edit&section=1 "Modifier la section : Nom") \]

Les prénoms de Clausewitz sont parfois donnés dans les sources non allemandes comme « Karl », « Carl Philipp Gottlieb » ou « Carl Maria ». Il a orthographié son propre prénom avec un « C » afin de s'identifier à la tradition occidentale classique ; les auteurs qui utilisent « Karl » cherchent souvent à souligner leur identité allemande (plutôt qu'européenne). « Carl Philipp Gottfried » figure sur la pierre tombale de Clausewitz. <sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;">[</font></font></span><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"> 3 </font></font><span class="cite-bracket"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;">]</font></font></span></a></sup> Néanmoins, des sources comme l'historien militaire [Peter Paret](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Paret "Pierre Paret") et *[l'Encyclopædia Britannica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica "Encyclopédie Britannica")* continuent d'utiliser Gottlieb au lieu de Gottfried. <sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;">[</font></font></span><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"> 4 </font></font><span class="cite-bracket"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;">]</font></font></span></a></sup>

## Vie et carrière militaire

\[ [modifier](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carl_von_Clausewitz&action=edit&section=2 "Modifier la section : Vie et carrière militaire") \]

Clausewitz est né le 1er juillet 1780 à [Burg bei Magdeburg, dans le](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burg_bei_Magdeburg "Château près de Magdebourg") [duché](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duchy_of_Magdeburg "Duché de Magdebourg") prussien de Magdebourg . Il était le quatrième et dernier fils d'une famille qui revendiquait un statut noble, accepté par Charles. La famille de Clausewitz prétendait descendre des barons de Clausewitz en [Haute-Silésie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upper_Silesia "Haute-Silésie") , bien que les chercheurs s'interrogent sur ce lien. <sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;">[</font></font></span><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"> 5 </font></font><span class="cite-bracket"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;">]</font></font></span></a></sup> Son grand-père, fils d'un pasteur [luthérien](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lutheranism "luthéranisme") , avait été professeur de théologie. Le père de Clausewitz, ancien lieutenant dans l'armée de [Frédéric le Grand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frederick_the_Great "Frédéric le Grand") , [roi de Prusse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_monarchs_of_Prussia "Liste des monarques de Prusse") , occupait un poste subalterne au service des impôts intérieurs prussiens. Clausewitz entra dans l'armée prussienne à l'âge de douze ans comme [caporal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lance_corporal "Caporal") , atteignant finalement le grade de général de division. <sup id="cite_ref-ClausewitzDotComBio_7-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-ClausewitzDotComBio-7"><span class="cite-bracket"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;">[</font></font></span><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"> 6 </font></font><span class="cite-bracket"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;">]</font></font></span></a></sup>

Clausewitz a servi dans les [campagnes du Rhin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_of_the_First_Coalition "Guerre de la Première Coalition") (1793-1794) y compris le [siège de Mayence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Mainz_\(1793\) "Siège de Mayence (1793)") , lorsque l' [armée prussienne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prussian_Army "Armée prussienne") a envahi la France pendant la [Révolution française](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Revolution "Révolution française") , et a combattu dans les [guerres napoléoniennes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars "Guerres napoléoniennes") de 1806 à 1815. Il est entré à la *[Kriegsakademie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prussian_Staff_College "Collège d'état-major prussien")* (également citée comme « l'école de guerre allemande », « l'Académie militaire de Berlin » et « l'Académie militaire prussienne », plus tard le « Collège de guerre ») à Berlin en 1801 (à l'âge de 21 ans), a probablement étudié les écrits des philosophes [Emmanuel Kant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immanuel_Kant "Emmanuel Kant") et/ou [Johann Gottlieb Fichte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Gottlieb_Fichte "Johann Gottlieb Fichte") et [Friedrich Schleiermacher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Schleiermacher "Friedrich Schleiermacher") et a gagné l'estime du général [Gerhard von Scharnhorst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gerhard_von_Scharnhorst "Gerhard von Scharnhorst") , le futur premier chef d'état-major de l'armée prussienne nouvellement réformée (nommé en 1809). Clausewitz, [Hermann von Boyen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_von_Boyen "Hermann von Boyen") (1771–1848) et [Karl von Grolman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_von_Grolman "Karl von Grolman") (1777–1843) furent parmi les principaux alliés de Scharnhorst dans ses efforts pour réformer l'armée prussienne entre 1807 et 1814. <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;">[ </font></font><i><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipédia&nbsp;: citation nécessaire"><span title="Cette affirmation nécessite des références à des sources fiables. (Juin 2017)"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;">citation nécessaire</font></font></span></a></i><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"><font style="vertical-align: inherit;"> ]</font></font></sup>

Clausewitz served during the Jena Campaign as [aide-de-camp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aide-de-camp "Aide de camp") to Prince [August](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prince_Augustus_Ferdinand_of_Prussia "Prince Auguste Ferdinand de Prusse"). At the [Battle of Jena-Auerstedt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Jena-Auerstedt "Bataille d'Iéna-Auerstedt") on 14 October 1806—when Napoleon invaded Prussia and defeated the Prussian-Saxon army commanded by [Karl Wilhelm Ferdinand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_William_Ferdinand "Charles Guillaume Ferdinand"), [Duke of Brunswick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duchy_of_Brunswick "Duché de Brunswick")—he was captured, one of the 25,000 prisoners taken that day as the Prussian army disintegrated. He was 26. Clausewitz was held prisoner with his prince in [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France "France") from 1807 to 1808. Returning to Prussia, he assisted in the reform of the Prussian army and state.<sup id="cite_ref-ClausewitzDotComBio_7-1" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-ClausewitzDotComBio-7"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> Johann Gottlieb Fichte wrote *On Machiavelli, as an Author, and Passages from His Writings* in June 1807. ("*Über Machiavell, als Schriftsteller, und Stellen aus seinen Schriften*" ). Carl Clausewitz wrote an interesting and anonymous Letter to Fichte (1809) about his book on *Machiavelli.* The letter was published in Fichte's *Verstreute kleine Schriften* 157–166. For an English translation of the letter see *Carl von Clausewitz Historical and Political Writings* Edited by: Peter Paret and D. Moran (1992).

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Marievonclausewitz.jpg/250px-Marievonclausewitz.jpg)

Marie von Clausewitz (née, Countess von Brühl)

On 10 December 1810, he married the socially prominent [Countess](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Countess "Comtesse") [Marie von Brühl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marie_von_Br%C3%BChl "Marie von Brühl"), whom he had first met in 1803. She was a member of the noble German [Brühl family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Br%C3%BChl_family "Famille Brühl") originating in [Thuringia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thuringia "Thuringe"). The couple moved in the highest circles, socialising with Berlin's political, literary, and intellectual élite. Marie was well-educated and politically well-connected—she played an important role in her husband's career progress and intellectual evolution.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> She also edited, published, and introduced his collected works.<sup id="cite_ref-5_Things_9-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-5_Things-9"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>

Opposed to Prussia's enforced alliance with [Napoleon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon "Napoléon"), Clausewitz left the Prussian army and served in the [Imperial Russian Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_Russian_Army "Armée impériale russe") from 1812 to 1813 during the [Russian campaign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_invasion_of_Russia "Invasion française de la Russie"), taking part in the [Battle of Borodino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Borodino "Bataille de Borodino") (1812). Like many Prussian officers serving in Russia, he joined the [Russian–German Legion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian%E2%80%93German_Legion "Légion russo-allemande") in 1813. In the service of the [Russian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Empire russe"), Clausewitz helped negotiate the [Convention of Tauroggen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convention_of_Tauroggen "Convention de Tauroggen") (1812), which prepared the way for the coalition of Prussia, Russia, and the [United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland "Royaume-Uni de Grande-Bretagne et d'Irlande") that ultimately defeated Napoleon and his allies.<sup id="cite_ref-ClausewitzDotComBio_7-2" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-ClausewitzDotComBio-7"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>

In 1815 the Russian-German Legion became integrated into the Prussian Army and Clausewitz re-entered Prussian service as a colonel.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> He was soon appointed chief-of-staff of [Johann von Thielmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_von_Thielmann "Johann von Thielmann")'s III Corps. In that capacity he served at the [Battle of Ligny](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Ligny "Bataille de Ligny") and the [Battle of Wavre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Wavre "Bataille de Wavre") during the [Waterloo campaign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waterloo_campaign "Campagne de Waterloo") in 1815. An army led personally by Napoleon defeated the Prussians at [Ligny](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ligny "Ligny") (south of [Mont-Saint-Jean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mont-Saint-Jean,_Belgium "Mont-Saint-Jean, Belgique") and the village of [Waterloo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waterloo,_Belgium "Waterloo, Belgique")) on 16 June 1815, but they withdrew in good order. Napoleon's failure to destroy the Prussian forces led to his defeat a few days later at the [Battle of Waterloo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Waterloo "Bataille de Waterloo") (18 June 1815), when the Prussian forces arrived on his right flank late in the afternoon to support the Anglo-Dutch-Belgian forces pressing his front. Napoleon had convinced his troops that the field grey uniforms were those of Marshal Grouchy's grenadiers. Clausewitz's unit fought heavily outnumbered at [Wavre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Wavre "Bataille de Wavre") (18–19 June 1815), preventing large reinforcements from reaching Napoleon at Waterloo. After the war, Clausewitz served as the director of the *Kriegsakademie*, where he served until 1830. In that year he returned to active duty with the army. Soon afterward, the outbreak of several revolutions around Europe and [a crisis in Poland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/November_Uprising "Soulèvement de novembre") appeared to presage another major European war. Clausewitz was appointed chief of staff of the only army Prussia was able to mobilise in this emergency, which was sent to the Polish border. Its commander, [Gneisenau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/August_Neidhardt_von_Gneisenau "August Neidhardt von Gneisenau"), died of [cholera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cholera "Choléra") (August 1831), and Clausewitz took command of the Prussian army's efforts to construct a *cordon sanitaire* to contain [the great cholera outbreak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1826%E2%80%931837_cholera_pandemic#Origins_of_the_second_pandemic "Pandémie de choléra de 1826-1837") (the first time cholera had appeared in modern heartland Europe, causing a continent-wide panic). Clausewitz himself died of the same disease shortly afterwards, on 16 November 1831.<sup id="cite_ref-ClausewitzDotComBio_7-3" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-ClausewitzDotComBio-7"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>

His widow edited, published, and wrote the introduction to his *[magnum opus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masterpiece "Chef-d'œuvre")* on the [philosophy of war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy_of_war "Philosophie de la guerre") in 1832. (He had started working on the text in 1816 but had not completed it.)<sup id="cite_ref-smith57_11-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-smith57-11"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> She wrote the preface for *On War* and had published most of his collected works by 1835.<sup id="cite_ref-5_Things_9-1" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-5_Things-9"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> She died in January 1836.

## Theory of war

\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carl_von_Clausewitz&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Theory of war")\]

Clausewitz was a professional combat soldier and a staff officer<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> who was involved in numerous military campaigns, but he is famous primarily as a military theorist interested in the examination of war, utilising the campaigns of [Frederick the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frederick_the_Great "Frederick the Great") and [Napoleon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon "Napoleon") as frames of reference for his work.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> He wrote a careful, systematic, philosophical examination of war in all its aspects. The result was his principal book, *[On War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On_War "On War")*, a major work on the philosophy of war. It was unfinished when Clausewitz died and contains material written at different stages in his intellectual evolution, producing some significant contradictions between different sections. The sequence and precise character of that evolution is a source of much debate as to the exact meaning behind some seemingly contradictory observations in discussions pertinent to the tactical, operational and strategic levels of war, for example (though many of these apparent contradictions are simply the result of his dialectical method). Clausewitz constantly sought to revise the text, particularly between 1827 and his departure on his last field assignments, to include more material on "people's war" and forms of war other than high-intensity warfare between states, but relatively little of this material was included in the book.<sup id="cite_ref-smith57_11-1" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-smith57-11"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> Soldiers before this time had written treatises on various military subjects, but none had undertaken a great philosophical examination of war on the scale of those written by Clausewitz and [Leo Tolstoy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leo_Tolstoy "Leo Tolstoy"), both of whom were inspired by the events of the [Napoleonic Era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleonic_Era "Napoleonic Era").

Clausewitz's work is still studied today, demonstrating its continued relevance. More than sixteen major English-language books that focused specifically on his work were published between 2005 and 2014, whereas his 19th-century rival [Jomini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antoine-Henri_Jomini "Antoine-Henri Jomini") has faded from influence. The historian [Lynn Montross](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lynn_Montross "Lynn Montross") said that this outcome "may be explained by the fact that Jomini produced a system of war, Clausewitz a philosophy. The one has been outdated by new weapons, the other still influences the strategy behind those weapons."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> Jomini did not attempt to define war but Clausewitz did, providing (and dialectically comparing) a number of definitions. The first is his dialectical thesis: "War is thus an act of force to compel our enemy to do our will." The second, often treated as Clausewitz's 'bottom line,' is in fact merely his dialectical antithesis: "War is merely the continuation of policy with other means." The synthesis of his dialectical examination of the nature of war is his famous "trinity," saying that war is "a fascinating trinity—composed of primordial violence, hatred, and enmity, which are to be regarded as a blind natural force; the play of chance and probability, within which the creative spirit is free to roam; and its element of subordination, as an instrument of policy, which makes it subject to pure reason."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Synthesis_of_published_material" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag may be based upon an improper synthesis of sources. (October 2020)">improper synthesis?</span></a></i>]</sup> Christopher Bassford says the best shorthand for Clausewitz's trinity should be something like "violent emotion/chance/rational calculation." However, it is frequently presented as "people/army/government," a misunderstanding based on a later paragraph in the same section. This misrepresentation was popularised by U.S. Army Colonel [Harry Summers'](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harry_G._Summers_Jr. "Harry G. Summers Jr.") Vietnam-era interpretation,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> facilitated by weaknesses in the 1976 Howard/Paret translation.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>

The degree to which Clausewitz managed to revise his manuscript to reflect that synthesis is the subject of much debate. His final reference to war and *Politik*, however, goes beyond his widely quoted antithesis: "War is simply the continuation of political intercourse with the addition of other means. We deliberately use the phrase 'with the addition of other means' because we also want to make it clear that war in itself does not suspend political intercourse or change it into something entirely different. In essentials that intercourse continues, irrespective of the means it employs. The main lines along which military events progress, and to which they are restricted, are political lines that continue throughout the war into the subsequent peace."<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>

> A prince or general who knows exactly how to organise his war according to his object and means, who does neither too little nor too much, gives by that the greatest proof of his genius. But the effects of this talent are exhibited not so much by the invention of new modes of action, which might strike the eye immediately, as in the successful final result of the whole. It is the exact fulfilment of silent suppositions, it is the noiseless harmony of the whole action which we should admire, and which only makes itself known in the total result.
> 
> — Clausewitz, *On War*, Book III, Chapter 1<sup id="cite_ref-OnWar1873_19-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-OnWar1873-19"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: Vol. I pgs. 85–86">: Vol. I pgs. 85–86 </span></sup> 

Clausewitz introduced systematic philosophical contemplation into Western military thinking, with powerful implications not only for historical and analytical writing but also for practical policy, military instruction, and operational planning. He relied on his own experiences, contemporary writings about Napoleon, and on deep historical research. His historiographical approach is evident in his first extended study, written when he was 25, of the [Thirty Years' War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War "Thirty Years' War"). In *On War*, Clausewitz sees all wars as the sum of decisions, actions, and reactions in an uncertain and dangerous context, and also as a socio-political phenomenon. He also stressed the complex nature of war, which encompasses both the socio-political and the operational and stresses the primacy of state policy. (One should be careful not to limit his observations on war to war between states, however, as he certainly discusses other kinds of protagonists).<sup id="cite_ref-Paret_20-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-Paret-20"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: viii">: viii </span></sup>  Clausewitz, according to [Azar Gat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azar_Gat "Azar Gat"), expressed in the field of military theory the main themes of the [Romantic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanticism "Romanticism") reaction against the worldview of the [Enlightenment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment "Age of Enlightenment"), rejecting universal principles and stressing historical diversity and the forces of the human spirit. This explains the strength and value of many of his arguments, derived from this great cultural movement, but also his often harsh rhetoric against his predecessors.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_21-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-:2-21"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_22-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-:1-22"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>

The word "[strategy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strategy "Strategy")" had only recently come into usage in modern Europe, and Clausewitz's definition is quite narrow: "the use of engagements for the object of war" (which many today would call "the operational level" of war). Clausewitz conceived of war as a political, social, and military phenomenon which might—depending on circumstances—involve the entire population of a political entity at war. In any case, Clausewitz saw military force as an instrument that states and other political actors use to pursue the ends of their policy, in a dialectic between opposing wills, each with the aim of imposing his policies and will upon his enemy.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>

Clausewitz's emphasis on the inherent superiority of the defense suggests that habitual aggressors are likely to end up as failures. The inherent superiority of the defense obviously does not mean that the defender will always win, however: there are other asymmetries to be considered. He was interested in co-operation between the regular army and militia or partisan forces, or citizen soldiers, as one possible—sometimes the only—method of defense. In the circumstances of the Wars of the French Revolution and those with Napoleon, which were energised by a rising spirit of nationalism, he emphasised the need for states to involve their entire populations in the conduct of war. This point is especially important, as these wars demonstrated that such energies could be of decisive importance and for a time led to a democratisation of the armed forces much as [universal suffrage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_suffrage "Universal suffrage") democratised politics.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>

While Clausewitz was intensely aware of the value of intelligence at all levels, he was also very skeptical of the accuracy of much military intelligence: "Many intelligence reports in war are contradictory; even more are false, and most are uncertain.... In short, most intelligence is false."<sup id="cite_ref-OnWar1873_19-1" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-OnWar1873-19"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: Vol. I p. 38">: Vol. I p. 38 </span></sup>  This circumstance is generally described as part of the fog of war. Such skeptical comments apply only to intelligence at the tactical and operational levels; at the strategic and political levels he constantly stressed the requirement for the best possible understanding of what today would be called strategic and political intelligence. His conclusions were influenced by his experiences in the Prussian Army, which was often in an intelligence fog due partly to the superior abilities of Napoleon's system but even more simply to the nature of war. Clausewitz acknowledges that friction creates enormous difficulties for the realization of any plan, and the *fog of war* hinders commanders from knowing what is happening. It is precisely in the context of this challenge that he develops the concept of **military genius** ([German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_language "German language"): *der kriegerische Genius*), evidenced above all in the execution of operations. 'Military genius' is not simply a matter of intellect, but a combination of qualities of intellect, experience, personality, and temperament (and there are many possible such combinations) that create a very highly developed mental aptitude for the waging of war.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-25"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>

### Principal ideas

\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carl_von_Clausewitz&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Principal ideas")\]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/CarlvonClausewitz.jpg/250px-CarlvonClausewitz.jpg)

Clausewitz as a young man

Key ideas discussed in *On War* include:<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>

- the [dialectical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialectic "Dialectic") approach to military analysis
- the methods of "critical analysis"
- the economic profit-seeking logic of commercial enterprise is equally applicable to the waging of war and negotiating for peace
- the nature of the balance-of-power mechanism
- the relationship between political objectives and military objectives in war
- the asymmetrical relationship between attack and defense
- the nature of "military genius" (involving matters of personality and character, beyond intellect)
- the "fascinating trinity" (*wunderliche Dreifaltigkeit*) of war<sup id="cite_ref-TipToe_27-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-TipToe-27"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>
- philosophical distinctions between "[absolute war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_war "Absolute war")," "ideal war," and "real war"
- in "real war," the distinctive poles of a) limited objectives (political and/or military) and b) war to "render the enemy helpless"
- the idea that war and its conduct belong fundamentally to the social realm rather than to the realms of art or science
- "strategy" belongs primarily to the realm of art, but is constrained by quantitative analyses of political benefits versus military costs & losses
- "tactics" belongs primarily to the realm of science (most obvious in the development of siege warfare)
- the importance of "moral forces" (more than simply "morale") as opposed to quantifiable physical elements
- the "military virtues" of professional armies (which do not necessarily trump the rather different virtues of other kinds of fighting forces)
- conversely, the very real effects of a superiority in numbers and "mass"
- the essential unpredictability of war
- the "fog of war"<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-28"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>note 2<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>
- "friction" – the disparity between the ideal performance of units, organisations or systems and their actual performance in real-world scenarios (Book I, Chapter VII)
- strategic and operational "[centers of gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_gravity_\(military\) "Center of gravity (military)")"<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-29"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>note 3<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>
- the "[culminating point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culminating_point "Culminating point") of the offensive"
- the "culminating point of victory"

## Interpretation and misinterpretation

\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carl_von_Clausewitz&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Interpretation and misinterpretation")\]

Clausewitz used a [dialectical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialectical "Dialectical") method to construct his argument, leading to frequent misinterpretation of his ideas. British military theorist [B. H. Liddell Hart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B._H._Liddell_Hart "B. H. Liddell Hart") contends that the enthusiastic acceptance by the [Prussian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prussia "Prussia") military establishment—especially [Moltke the Elder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helmuth_von_Moltke_the_Elder "Helmuth von Moltke the Elder"), a former student of Clausewitz<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-30"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>—of what they believed to be Clausewitz's ideas, and the subsequent widespread adoption of the Prussian military system worldwide, had a deleterious effect on [military theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_theory "Military theory") and [practice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warfare "Warfare"), due to their egregious misinterpretation of his ideas:

> As so often happens, Clausewitz's disciples carried his teaching to an extreme which their master had not intended.... \[Clausewitz's\] theory of war was expounded in a way too abstract and involved for ordinary soldier-minds, essentially concrete, to follow the course of his argument—which often turned back from the direction in which it was apparently leading. Impressed yet befogged, they grasped at his vivid leading phrases, seeing only their surface meaning, and missing the deeper current of his thought.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-31"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>

As described by [Christopher Bassford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christopher_Bassford "Christopher Bassford"), then-professor of strategy at the [National War College](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_War_College "National War College") of the United States:

> One of the main sources of confusion about Clausewitz's approach lies in his dialectical method of presentation. For example, Clausewitz's famous line that "War is the continuation of policy with other means," ("*Der Krieg ist eine bloße Fortsetzung der Politik mit anderen Mitteln*") while accurate as far as it goes, was not intended as a statement of fact. It is the antithesis in a dialectical argument whose thesis is the point—made earlier in the analysis—that "war is nothing but a duel \[or wrestling match, the extended metaphor in which that discussion was embedded\] on a larger scale." His synthesis, which resolves the deficiencies of these two bold statements, says that war is neither "nothing but" an act of brute force nor "merely" a rational act of politics or policy. This synthesis lies in his "fascinating trinity" \[*wunderliche Dreifaltigkeit*\]: a dynamic, inherently unstable interaction of the forces of violent emotion, chance, and rational calculation.<sup id="cite_ref-ClausewitzDotComBio_7-4" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-ClausewitzDotComBio-7"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>

Another example of this confusion is the idea that Clausewitz was a proponent of [total war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_war "Total war") as used in the Third Reich's propaganda in the 1940s. In fact, Clausewitz never used the term "total war": rather, he discussed "absolute war," a concept which evolved into the much more abstract notion of "ideal war" discussed at the very beginning of *Vom Kriege*—the purely *logical* result of the forces underlying a "pure," Platonic "ideal" of war.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-32"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> In what he called a "logical fantasy," war cannot be waged in a limited way: the rules of competition will force participants to use all means at their disposal to achieve victory. But in the *real world,* he said, such rigid logic is unrealistic and dangerous. As a practical matter, the military objectives in *real* war that support political objectives generally fall into two broad types: limited aims or the effective "disarming" of the enemy "to render \[him\] politically helpless or militarily impotent. Thus, the complete defeat of the enemy may not be necessary, desirable, or even possible.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-33"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>

According to [Azar Gat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azar_Gat "Azar Gat"), the opposing interpretations of Clausewitz are rooted in Clausewitz’s own conceptual journey.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_21-1" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-:2-21"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_22-1" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-:1-22"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> The centerpiece of Clausewitz’s theory of war throughout his life was his concept of all-out fighting and energetic conduct leading to the great battle of annihilation. He believed such conduct expressed the very “nature”, or “lasting spirit” of war. Accordingly, Clausewitz disparaged the significance of the [maneuver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maneuver_warfare "Maneuver warfare"), surprise, and cunning in war, as distracting from the centrality of [battle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle "Battle"),<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-34"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> and argued that defense was legitimate only if and as long as one was weaker than the enemy.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-35"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> Nevertheless, in the last years of his life, after the first six out of the eight books of *On War* had already been drafted, Clausewitz came to recognize that this concept was not universal and did not even apply to the [Napoleonic Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars "Napoleonic Wars"), the supreme model of his theory of war. This was demonstrated by the [Spanish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peninsular_War "Peninsular War") and [Russian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_invasion_of_Russia "French invasion of Russia") campaigns and by [guerrilla warfare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guerrilla_warfare "Guerrilla warfare"), in all of which battle was systematically avoided. Consequently, from 1827 on, Clausewitz recognized the legitimacy of [limited war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limited_war "Limited war") and explained it by the influence of politics that harnessed the unlimited nature of war to serve its objectives. Clausewitz died in 1831 before he completed the revision he planned along these lines. He incorporated his new ideas only into the end of Book VI, Book VIII and the beginning of Book I of *On War*. As a result, when published, *On War* encompassed both his old and new ideas, at odds with each other.

Thus, against common interpretations of *On War*, Gat points out that Clausewitz’s transformed views regarding the relationship between politics and war and the admission of limited war into his theory constituted a U-turn against his own life-long fundamental view of the nature of war. Gat further argues the readers’ miscomprehension of the theory in *On War* as complete and dialectical, rather than a draft undergoing a radical change of mind, has thus generated a range of reactions. People of each age have found in *On War* the Clausewitz who suited their own views on war and its conduct. Between 1870 and 1914, he was celebrated mainly for his insistence on the clash of forces and the decisive battle, and his emphasis on moral forces. By contrast, after 1945, during the [nuclear age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_Age "Atomic Age"), his reputation has reached a second pinnacle for his later acceptance of the primacy of politics and the concept of limited war.

Referring to much of the current interpretation of *On War* as the [Emperor’s New Clothes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Emperor%27s_New_Clothes "The Emperor's New Clothes") syndrome, Gat argues that instead of critically addressing the puzzling contradictions in *On War,* Clausewitz has been set in stone and could not be wrong.

In modern times the reconstruction of Clausewitzian theory has been a matter of much dispute. One analysis was that of [Panagiotis Kondylis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panagiotis_Kondylis "Panagiotis Kondylis"), a Greek writer and philosopher, who opposed the interpretations of [Raymond Aron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raymond_Aron "Raymond Aron") in *Penser la Guerre, Clausewitz,* and other liberal writers. According to Aron, Clausewitz was one of the first writers to condemn the militarism of the Prussian general staff and its war-proneness, based on Clausewitz's argument that "war is a continuation of policy by other means." In *Theory of War,* Kondylis claims that this is inconsistent with Clausewitzian thought. He claims that Clausewitz was morally indifferent to war (though this probably reflects a lack of familiarity with personal letters from Clausewitz, which demonstrate an acute awareness of war's tragic aspects) and that his advice regarding politics' dominance over the conduct of war has nothing to do with pacifist ideas.

Other notable writers who have studied Clausewitz's texts and translated them into English are historians [Peter Paret](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Paret "Peter Paret") of the [Institute for Advanced Study](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institute_for_Advanced_Study "Institute for Advanced Study") and [Sir Michael Howard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Howard_\(historian\) "Michael Howard (historian)"). Howard and Paret edited the most widely used edition of *On War* (Princeton University Press, 1976/1984) and have produced comparative studies of Clausewitz and other theorists, such as Tolstoy. [Bernard Brodie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernard_Brodie_\(military_strategist\) "Bernard Brodie (military strategist)")'s *A Guide to the Reading of "On War,"* in the 1976 Princeton translation, expressed his interpretations of the Prussian's theories and provided students with an influential synopsis of this vital work. The 1873 translation by Colonel James John Graham was heavily—and controversially—edited by the philosopher, musician, and [game theorist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Game_theory "Game theory") [Anatol Rapoport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatol_Rapoport "Anatol Rapoport").

The British military historian [John Keegan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Keegan "John Keegan") attacked Clausewitz's theory in his book *[A History of Warfare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_History_of_Warfare "A History of Warfare")*.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-36"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> Keegan argued that Clausewitz assumed the existence of states, yet 'war antedates the state, diplomacy and strategy by many millennia.'

## Influence

\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carl_von_Clausewitz&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Influence")\]

Clausewitz died without completing *Vom Kriege,* but despite this his ideas have been widely influential in [military theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_theory "Military theory") and have had a strong influence on German military thought specifically. Later Prussian and German generals, such as [Helmuth Graf von Moltke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helmuth_von_Moltke_the_Elder "Helmuth von Moltke the Elder"), were clearly influenced by Clausewitz: Moltke's widely quoted statement that "No operational plan extends with high certainty beyond the first encounter with the main enemy force" is a classic reflection of Clausewitz's insistence on the roles of chance, friction, "fog," uncertainty, and interactivity in war.<sup id="cite_ref-ClausewitzInEnglish_37-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-ClausewitzInEnglish-37"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 20–21">: 20–21 </span></sup> 

Clausewitz's influence spread to British thinking as well, though at first more as a historian and analyst than as a theorist.<sup id="cite_ref-ClausewitzInEnglish_37-1" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-ClausewitzInEnglish-37"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> See for example Wellington's extended essay discussing Clausewitz's study of the Campaign of 1815—Wellington's only serious written discussion of the battle, which was widely discussed in 19th-century Britain. Clausewitz's broader thinking came to the fore following Britain's military embarrassments in the Boer War (1899–1902). One example of a heavy Clausewitzian influence in that era is [Spenser Wilkinson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spenser_Wilkinson "Spenser Wilkinson"), journalist, the first [Chichele Professor of Military History](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chichele_Professor_of_Military_History "Chichele Professor of Military History") at Oxford University, and perhaps the most prominent military analyst in Britain from c. 1885 until well into the interwar period. Another is naval historian [Julian Corbett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_Corbett "Julian Corbett") (1854–1922), whose work reflected a deep if idiosyncratic adherence to Clausewitz's concepts and frequently an emphasis on Clausewitz's ideas about 'limited objectives' and the inherent strengths of the defensive form of war. Corbett's practical strategic views were often in prominent public conflict with Wilkinson's—see, for example, Wilkinson's article "Strategy at Sea", *The Morning Post*, 12 February 1912. Following the First World War, however, the influential British military commentator [B. H. Liddell Hart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B._H._Liddell_Hart "B. H. Liddell Hart") in the 1920s erroneously attributed to him the doctrine of "total war" that during the First World War had been embraced by many European general staffs and emulated by the British. More recent scholars typically see that war as so confused in terms of political rationale that it in fact contradicts much of *On War.*<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-38"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> That view assumes, however, a set of values as to what constitutes "rational" political objectives—in this case, values not shaped by the fervid [Social Darwinism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_Darwinism "Social Darwinism") that was rife in 1914 Europe. One of the most influential British Clausewitzians today is [Colin S. Gray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colin_S._Gray "Colin S. Gray"); historian [Hew Strachan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hew_Strachan "Hew Strachan") (like Wilkinson also the [Chichele Professor of Military History](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chichele_Professor_of_Military_History "Chichele Professor of Military History") at Oxford University, since 2001) has been an energetic proponent of the *study* of Clausewitz, but his own views on Clausewitz's ideas are somewhat ambivalent.

With some interesting exceptions (e.g., [John McAuley Palmer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_McAuley_Palmer_\(general\) "John McAuley Palmer (general)"), [Robert M. Johnston](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Matteson_Johnston "Robert Matteson Johnston"), Hoffman Nickerson), Clausewitz had little influence on American military thought before 1945 other than via British writers, though Generals [Eisenhower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eisenhower "Eisenhower") and [Patton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patton "Patton") were avid readers of English translations. He did influence [Karl Marx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Marx "Karl Marx"), [Friedrich Engels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Engels "Friedrich Engels"), [Vladimir Lenin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin "Vladimir Lenin"), [Leon Trotsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leon_Trotsky "Leon Trotsky"),<sup id="cite_ref-Cormier_39-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-Cormier-39"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> <sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 233–260">: 233–260 </span></sup>  [Võ Nguyên Giáp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V%C3%B5_Nguy%C3%AAn_Gi%C3%A1p "Võ Nguyên Giáp"),<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-40"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> [Ferdinand Foch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferdinand_Foch "Ferdinand Foch"),<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-41"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> and [Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong"), and thus the Communist Soviet and Chinese traditions, as Lenin emphasized the inevitability of wars among capitalist states in the age of imperialism and presented the armed struggle of the working class as the only path toward the eventual elimination of war.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-42"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> Because [Lenin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin "Vladimir Lenin") was an admirer of Clausewitz and called him "one of the great military writers," his influence on the Red Army was immense.<sup id="cite_ref-Mertsalov,_A.N._pages_11-19_43-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-Mertsalov,_A.N._pages_11-19-43"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> The Russian historian A.N. Mertsalov commented that "It was an irony of fate that the view in the USSR was that it was Lenin who shaped the attitude towards Clausewitz, and that Lenin's dictum that war is a continuation of politics is taken from the work of this \[allegedly\] anti-humanist anti-revolutionary."<sup id="cite_ref-Mertsalov,_A.N._pages_11-19_43-1" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-Mertsalov,_A.N._pages_11-19-43"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> The American mathematician [Anatol Rapoport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatol_Rapoport "Anatol Rapoport") wrote in 1968 that Clausewitz as interpreted by Lenin formed the basis of all Soviet military thinking since 1917, and quoted the remarks by [Marshal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marshal_of_the_Soviet_Union "Marshal of the Soviet Union") [V.D. Sokolovsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vasily_Sokolovsky "Vasily Sokolovsky"):

> In describing the essence of war, Marxism-Leninism takes as its point of departure the premise that war is not an aim in itself, but rather a tool of politics. In his remarks on Clausewitz's *On War,* Lenin stressed that "Politics is the reason, and war is only the tool, not the other way around. Consequently, it remains only to subordinate the military point of view to the political."<sup id="cite_ref-Rapoport_44-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-Rapoport-44"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 37">: 37 </span></sup> 

[Henry A. Kissinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_A._Kissinger "Henry A. Kissinger"), however, described Lenin's approach as being that politics is a continuation of war by other means, thus turning Clausewitz's argument "on its head."<sup id="cite_ref-ClausewitzInEnglish_37-2" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-ClausewitzInEnglish-37"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 198">: 198 </span></sup> 

Rapoport argued that:

> As for Lenin's approval of Clausewitz, it probably stems from his obsession with the struggle for power. The whole Marxist conception of history is that of successive struggles for power, primarily between social classes. This was constantly applied by Lenin in a variety of contexts. Thus the entire history of philosophy appears in Lenin's writings as a vast struggle between "idealism" and "materialism." The fate of the socialist movement was to be decided by a struggle between the revolutionists and the reformers. Clausewitz's acceptance of the struggle for power as the essence of international politics must have impressed Lenin as starkly realistic.<sup id="cite_ref-Rapoport_44-1" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-Rapoport-44"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 37–38">: 37–38 </span></sup> 

Clausewitz directly influenced Mao Zedong, who read *On War* in 1938 and organised a seminar on Clausewitz for the Party leadership in [Yan'an](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yan%27an "Yan'an"). Thus the "Clausewitzian" content in many of Mao's writings is not merely a regurgitation of Lenin but reflects Mao's own study.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-45"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> The idea that war involves inherent "friction" that distorts, to a greater or lesser degree, all prior arrangements, has become common currency in fields such as business strategy and sport. The phrase *fog of war* derives from Clausewitz's stress on how confused warfare can seem while one is immersed within it.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-46"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> The term [center of gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_gravity_\(military\) "Center of gravity (military)"), used in a military context derives from Clausewitz's usage, which he took from [Newtonian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newtonian_mechanics "Newtonian mechanics"). In U.S. military doctrine, "center of gravity" refers to the basis of an opponent's power at the operational, strategic, or political level, though this is only one aspect of Clausewitz's use of the term.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-47"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>44<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>

### Late 20th and early 21st century

\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carl_von_Clausewitz&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Late 20th and early 21st century")\]

The deterrence strategy of the United States in the 1950s was closely inspired by President [Dwight Eisenhower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwight_Eisenhower "Dwight Eisenhower")'s reading of Clausewitz as a young officer in the 1920s. Eisenhower was greatly impressed by Clausewitz's example of a theoretical, idealized "absolute war" in *Vom Kriege* as a way of demonstrating how absurd it would be to attempt such a strategy in practice. For Eisenhower, the age of nuclear weapons had made what was for Clausewitz in the early-19th century only a theoretical vision an all too real possibility in the mid-20th century. From Eisenhower's viewpoint, the best deterrent to war was to show the world just how appalling and horrific a nuclear "absolute war" would be if it should ever occur, hence a series of much-publicized nuclear tests in the Pacific, giving first priority in the defense budget to nuclear weapons and to their delivery-systems over conventional weapons, and making repeated statements in public that the United States was able and willing at all times to use nuclear weapons. In this way, through the [massive retaliation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massive_retaliation "Massive retaliation") doctrine and the closely related foreign-policy concept of [brinkmanship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brinkmanship "Brinkmanship"), Eisenhower hoped to hold out a credible vision of Clausewitzian nuclear "absolute war" in order to deter the Soviet Union and/or China from ever risking a war or even conditions that might lead to a war with the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-48"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>45<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>

> ...Philanthropists may easily imagine there is a skillful method of disarming and overcoming an enemy without causing great bloodshed, and that this is the proper tendency of the art of War. However plausible this may appear, still it is an error which must be extirpated; for in such dangerous things as war, the errors which proceed from a spirit of benevolence are just the worst. As the use of physical power to the utmost extent by no means excludes the co-operation of the intelligence, it follows that he who uses force unsparingly, without reference to the quantity of bloodshed, must obtain a superiority if his adversary does not act likewise. By such means the former dictates the law to the latter, and both proceed to extremities, to which the only limitations are those imposed by the amount of counteracting force on each side.
> 
> — Clausewitz, *On War*, Book I, Chapter 1<sup id="cite_ref-OnWar1873_19-4" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-OnWar1873-19"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: Vol. I, pp. 1–2">: Vol. I, pp. 1–2 </span></sup> 

After 1970, some theorists claimed that [nuclear proliferation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_proliferation "Nuclear proliferation") made Clausewitzian concepts obsolete after the 20th-century period in which they dominated the world.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-49"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>46<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> John E. Sheppard Jr., argues that by developing nuclear weapons, state-based conventional armies simultaneously both perfected their original purpose, to destroy a mirror image of themselves, and made themselves obsolete. No two [powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_powers "Nuclear powers") have used nuclear weapons against each other, instead using [diplomacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomacy "Diplomacy"), [conventional means](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conventional_warfare "Conventional warfare"), or [proxy wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxy_wars "Proxy wars") to settle disputes. If such a conflict did occur, presumably both combatants would be [annihilated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutually_assured_destruction "Mutually assured destruction"). Heavily influenced by the war in Vietnam and by antipathy to American strategist [Henry Kissinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Kissinger "Henry Kissinger"), the American biologist, musician, and game-theorist [Anatol Rapoport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatol_Rapoport "Anatol Rapoport") argued in 1968 that a Clausewitzian view of war was not only obsolete in the age of nuclear weapons, but also highly dangerous as it promoted a "zero-sum paradigm" to international relations and a "dissolution of rationality" amongst decision-makers.<sup id="cite_ref-Rapoport_44-2" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-Rapoport-44"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 73–77">: 73–77 </span></sup> 

The end of the 20th century and the beginning of the 21st century have seen many instances of state armies attempting to suppress [insurgencies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insurgency "Insurgency") and [terrorism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrorism "Terrorism"), and engaging in other forms of [asymmetrical warfare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asymmetrical_warfare "Asymmetrical warfare"). Clausewitz did not focus solely on wars between countries with well-defined armies. The era of the French Revolution and Napoleon was full of revolutions, rebellions, and violence by "non-state actors" - witness the wars in the French Vendée and in Spain. Clausewitz wrote a series of "Lectures on Small War" and studied the [rebellion in the Vendée](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_in_the_Vend%C3%A9e "War in the Vendée") (1793–1796) and the Tyrolean uprising of 1809. In his famous "Bekenntnisdenkschrift" of 1812 he called for a "Spanish war in Germany" and laid out a comprehensive guerrilla strategy to be waged against Napoleon. In *On War* he included a famous chapter on "The People in Arms".<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-50"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>47<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>

One prominent critic of Clausewitz is the Israeli military historian [Martin van Creveld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_van_Creveld "Martin van Creveld"). In his 1991 book *The Transformation of War*,<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-51"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>48<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> Creveld argued that Clausewitz's famous "Trinity" of people, army, and government was an obsolete socio-political construct based on the state, which was rapidly passing from the scene as the key player in war, and that he (Creveld) had constructed a new "non-trinitarian" model for modern warfare. Creveld's work has had great influence. Daniel Moran replied, 'The most egregious misrepresentation of Clausewitz's famous metaphor must be that of Martin van Creveld, who has declared Clausewitz to be an apostle of Trinitarian War, by which he means, incomprehensibly, a war of 'state against state and army against army,' from which the influence of the people is entirely excluded."<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-52"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>49<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> Christopher Bassford went further, noting that one need only *read* the paragraph in which Clausewitz defined his Trinity to see

> "that the words 'people,' 'army,' and 'government' appear nowhere at all in the list of the Trinity's components.... Creveld's and Keegan's assault on Clausewitz's Trinity is not only a classic 'blow into the air,' i.e., an assault on a position Clausewitz doesn't occupy. It is also a pointless attack on a concept that is quite useful in its own right. In any case, their failure to read the actual wording of the theory they so vociferously attack, and to grasp its deep relevance to the phenomena they describe, is hard to credit."<sup id="cite_ref-TipToe_27-1" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-TipToe-27"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>

Some have gone further and suggested that Clausewitz's best-known aphorism, that war is a continuation of policy with other means, is not only irrelevant today but also inapplicable historically.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-53"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>50<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup> For an opposing view see the sixteen essays presented in *Clausewitz in the Twenty-First Century* edited by [Hew Strachan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hew_Strachan "Hew Strachan") and Andreas Herberg-Rothe.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-54"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>51<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>

In military academies, schools, and universities worldwide, Clausewitz's *Vom Kriege* is often (usually in translation) mandatory reading.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-55"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>52<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>

Some theorists of [management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Management "Management") look to Clausewitz - just as some look to [Sun Tzu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun_Tzu "Sun Tzu") - to bolster ideas on the concept of [leadership](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leadership "Leadership").<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-56"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>53<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-57"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>54<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup>

1. **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_ref-1 "Jump up")** In [German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_language "German language") personal names, *[von](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Von "Von")* is a [preposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preposition "Preposition") which approximately means 'of' or 'from' and usually denotes some sort of [nobility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_nobility "German nobility"). While *von* (always lower case) is part of the family name or territorial designation, not a first or middle name, if the noble is referred to by their last name, use *[Schiller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Schiller "Friedrich Schiller")*, *Clausewitz* or *[Goethe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Wolfgang_von_Goethe "Johann Wolfgang von Goethe")*, not *von Schiller*, etc.
2. **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_ref-28 "Jump up")** "\[T\]he great uncertainty of all data in war is a peculiar difficulty, because all action must, to a certain extent, be planned in a mere twilight, which in addition not unfrequently—like the effect of a fog or moonshine—gives to things exaggerated dimensions and an unnatural appearance."<sup id="cite_ref-OnWar1873_19-2" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-OnWar1873-19"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: Vol. I p. 54">: Vol. I p. 54 </span></sup>
3. **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_ref-29 "Jump up")** "As the centre of gravity is always situated where the greatest mass of matter is collected, and as a shock against the center of gravity of a body always produces the greatest effect, and further, as the most effective blow is struck with the center of gravity of the power used, so it is also in war. The armed forces of every belligerent, whether of a single state or of an alliance of states, have a certain unity, and in that way, connection; but where connection is there come in analogies of the center of gravity. There are, therefore, in these armed forces certain centers of gravity, the movement and direction of which decide upon other points, and these centers of gravity are situated where the greatest bodies of troops are assembled. But just as, in the world of inert matter, the action against the center of gravity has its measure and limits in the connection of the parts, so it is in war, and here as well as there the force exerted may easily be greater than the resistance requires, and then there is a blow in the air, a waste of force."<sup id="cite_ref-OnWar1873_19-3" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/#cite_note-OnWar1873-19"><span class="cite-bracket">[</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: Vol. II p. 180">: Vol. II p. 180 </span></sup>
